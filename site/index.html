Regional hazard maps are two-dimensional, non-Gaussian random fields. Hazard Quantization distils an unlimited ensemble of intensity-measure maps into a small, optimally weighted set of representative scenarios via Functional Quantization by Infinite-Dimensional Centroidal Voronoi Tessellation (FQ-IDCVT). This is an interactive tour of the benchmark that asks one question: which clustering engine should drive the loop?

The headline result

Algorithm choice moves runtime by ~100×
— at negligible loss of accuracy

Every algorithm receives the same realizations and emits the same kind of output: N weighted quanta. They differ only in how they solve the Lloyd loop. The composite time–quality score (equal weights on speed and distortion, lower is better) collapses both dimensions into one ranking.

Composite time–quality trade-off score

Equal weights wt = wq = 0.5 · lower is better · CVT-achieving methods marked in green

Ultra-Fast CVT wins overall (0.089) by combining continuation warm-starts, random-projection dimensionality reduction and adaptive early stopping — its wall-clock time is essentially independent of resolution R. Among methods that provably reach a Centroidal Voronoi Tessellation, LK (Lloyd + k-means++) is the recommended default.

What is Hazard Quantization?

From an infinite ensemble to a handful of scenarios

①

Simulate

Draw Nsim synthetic intensity-measure maps — each a single realization of the regional hazard field.

②

Quantize

The FQ-IDCVT Lloyd loop clusters maps with similar shaking patterns, returning N representative quanta + probability masses.

③

Analyze

The weighted scenario set replaces the full ensemble as input to structural and network analyses — at a fraction of the cost.

The bottleneck is the iterative clustering loop. This study treats thirteen algorithms as drop-in replacements for it and measures exactly what you trade when you swap engines.
